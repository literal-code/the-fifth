Welcome to Retro-Igry. This is a site dedicated to retro games from the early 1980s to the late 1990s. Here you can find information and backstories to your favorite games, the impact they had on their respective genres and much more.

Sonic The Hedgehog, released by Sega in 1991, revolutionized platformers with its lightning-fast speed and momentum-based gameplay. Sonic's iconic blue character quickly became Sega's beloved mascot, rivaling Mario in popularity. The game's spin dash move and intricate level designs filled with loops and ramps added a dynamic element to the genre. Sonic's success led to numerous sequels, spin-offs, and a cultural impact that can still be felt today, inspiring modern platformers and solidifying its place as a legendary franchise. From its vibrant aesthetics to its rebellious attitude, Sonic The Hedgehog left an indelible mark on the platformer genre.
Super Mario Bros. revolutionized platformers in 1985, setting new standards. Mario became Nintendo's beloved mascot, symbolizing gaming worldwide. The game featured innovative side-scrolling gameplay, challenging levels, and power-ups. Intuitive controls, responsive mechanics, and imaginative power-ups added depth. Super Mario Bros. spawned a vast franchise, influencing the genre significantly.
DOOM, released in 1993, redefined the first-person shooter genre, leaving a lasting impact. The game introduced players to fast-paced, adrenaline-fueled gameplay and a relentless demon-slaying experience. DOOM's immersive graphics, haunting atmosphere, and intense sound design set new benchmarks for the genre. The game's level design encouraged exploration, secret hunting, and intense combat encounters. DOOM's success led to a cultural phenomenon, inspiring countless shooters and solidifying its place as a legendary franchise.
Half-Life, released in 1998, revolutionized the first-person shooter genre, leaving an enduring impact. The game introduced players to a captivating narrative, immersive atmosphere, and groundbreaking storytelling techniques. Half-Life's seamless gameplay integration and scripted events created a sense of realism and immersion rarely seen before. The game's innovative use of physics and puzzle-solving elements added depth and variety to the genre. Half-Life's success paved the way for narrative-driven shooters and influenced countless games, solidifying its status as a genre-defining classic.
The first Need for Speed, released in 1994, made a significant impact on the racing game genre, setting new standards for realism and immersion. The game introduced players to a thrilling street racing experience, featuring a variety of high-performance cars and intense competition. Need for Speed's impressive graphics, detailed car models, and realistic handling created an authentic racing atmosphere. The game's extensive customization options and police pursuit mechanics added depth and excitement to the gameplay. Need for Speed's success established it as a cornerstone of the racing game genre, inspiring numerous sequels and shaping the future of virtual car racing.
Command & Conquer 1, released in 1995, made a profound impact on the real-time strategy genre, setting new standards for depth and strategic gameplay. The game introduced players to a captivating storyline, featuring two factions locked in a global conflict, accompanied by immersive FMV cutscenes. Command & Conquer's innovative gameplay mechanics, such as base building, resource management, and unit control, offered a highly engaging and strategic experience. The game's diverse unit types, tactical options, and asymmetrical faction balance added depth and replayability to the genre. Command & Conquer's success paved the way for a renowned franchise, inspiring countless real-time strategy games and solidifying its place as a genre-defining classic.
